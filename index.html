    The maker

    Denise Gillioz

    atelier de Glaris

    Knitter by passion, storyteller by heart. Switzerland · since the spring of 2017.

    For thirty-eight years, I sat at administrative desks. I shuffled papers, kept ledgers, signed minutes. I did it well, and I did it gladly — but somewhere between the filing cabinets and the office windows, my hands had grown still. The day I retired, I cast on a pair of socks. I have not put the needles down since.

    Chapter one

    The retirement turn

    Retirement does not announce itself with fanfare. It arrives in a thank-you card and an empty Tuesday morning. For me, that morning was 14 March 2017. I made tea, opened the chalet shutters to a frost-bitten Tödi, and looked for something to hold.

    The needles came down from the attic — my mother’s, walnut, worn smooth by her own hands fifty years before. I cast on twenty stitches and did not look up until lunchtime. Something in me had been waiting for this quiet.

    “

    Each stitch is a small breath I take for the future.

    Denise Gillioz
    Chapter two

    Meeting wool

    I had knitted before — scarves for my grandsons, a cardigan once for a funeral I will not name. But meeting wool in retirement is not the same as meeting it as a hurried Sunday hobby.

    I read; I listened to spinners; I drove to small farms in Linthal and Klöntal where women whose names I now whisper like prayers — Mira, Ruth, Heidi — taught me how merino fibres want to be held, why baby alpaca lies down and merino springs up, what kid mohair does to the light at six in the evening. The wool taught me patience. Patience taught me to design.

    “

    Patience taught me to design.

    Denise Gillioz
    Chapter three

    The atelier today

    My atelier sits in the converted attic of our chalet, north-facing, lit by snow on six months of the year. There is a wood stove that I tend like a third grandchild, a worktable my father built in 1972, a vintage swift rescued from a Basel flea market.

    I knit between six in the morning and six in the evening, with a chamomile pause at noon. I host a monthly knit circle at Gemeindehausplatz; newcomers receive tea, a pattern, and a hand-written welcome.

    I do not sell. I share. I post photographs because I love the alpine palette and want it to live somewhere besides my own eyes. This site is not a shop — it is an open atelier. Come in. Sit a while.

    “

    I do not sell. I share.

    Denise Gillioz

    Quiet questions

    Before you write

    • Do you sell pieces from the gallery?

      No — the gallery is a sharing space. Pieces remain at the atelier or travel as gifts. If you fall in love with one, write to me; we may find a private way for it to live with you.

    • Can I commission a piece?

      Gentle, occasional commissions are possible — for a christening blanket, a wedding shawl, an heirloom mended into a new life. Tell me the story and the timeline; I will tell you whether the rows have room.

    • May I visit the atelier?

      Yes, by quiet appointment. Knit nights are open without notice. For private visits, write a few days ahead — kettle and chair await.

    • How long does a reply take?

      Within a week, often sooner. The atelier moves at the pace of stitches — never instantly, always sincerely.

    Threads of Resilience

    Rebuilding after difficult seasons with needles, wool, and the light of the Glarus Alps.

    05 Feb 2024 · 6 min read

    When a sudden diagnosis unsettled my family, knitting became my breathing space and the way I measured hope.

    I began each dawn by casting on ten slow rows, syncing breath to movement while the valley woke outside. Recording the texture of each yarn kept my thoughts present and softened anxious spirals.

    Resilience rows I rely on

    • Sync breath to stitches: inhale on knit, exhale on purl.
    • Pair every new yarn with a journal page capturing the memory it holds.
    • Take a midday walk in the valley before choosing the next colour.
    • Share a weekly progress snapshot with friends to celebrate small loops.

    These rituals turned projects into gentle anchors. Colours drawn from lake reflections and larch bark reminded me that change is seasonal, not final.

    “Knitting teaches me that progress is quiet; loop by loop we return to ourselves.”

    Now I invite neighbours and learners to join virtual knit nights from Glarus, weaving our stories together so no one feels alone with their needles.

    Comforting materials

    Current project

    Handspun Swiss merino held with baby alpaca on 4.5 mm wooden needles, palette inspired by the dawn over Tödi.

    I sip chamomile and lemon-balm tea while soft jazz keeps the rhythm steady and shoulders relaxed.

    Wool & Wellness Rituals

    Gentle maintenance routines that care for fibers and for the hands that craft them.

    22 Jan 2024 · 5 min read

    Caring for wool is an act of self-kindness; when I slow down with warm water and soap, my breathing follows.

    Prepare a mindful soak

    Fill a basin with lukewarm spring water, stir in a teaspoon of lanolin-rich soap, and let the fragrance of mountain pine rise before immersing the garment.

    Create a restorative finish

    Press fibres between towels with open palms, stretch gently on blocking mats, and allow your shoulders to drop as the piece relaxes.

    Wellness checklist

    • Light a beeswax candle to mark the care session as special.
    • Add a drop of lavender or pine for alpine calm.
    • Massage wrists and fingers while easing water from the wool.
    • Journal a sensory note once the piece is drying flat.

    These pauses keep garments luminous and remind me that maintenance is a space to listen inward, not a chore to rush.

    Aromatherapy palette

    • Cedar soaking board for grounding scent.
    • Eco wool wash with chamomile extract.
    • Microfiber towels reserved for knitwear only.
    • Blocking wires wrapped in linen tape for gentle hold.

    Weekly rhythm

    Alternate deep cleans with outdoor airing so fibres rest; give yourself the same alternating rhythm between effort and recovery.

    Contemplating Alpine Horizons

    A meditative walk through Glarus landscapes that colour my shawls and soothe the heart.

    10 Dec 2023 · 4 min read

    Some designs begin with silence. I board the train toward Linthal, sketching peaks as sunrise spills over the valley.

    Colour notes from the valley

    Frosted meadows offer silvery sage, while the Linth River gifts deep teal. I capture each hue in a palette notebook before it fades.

    Observation prompts

    Pause, name three colours you see, note how the air feels on your skin, and imagine which stitch texture could hold that feeling.

    Mindful travel companions

    A lightweight camera, an audio recorder of cowbells, and a pouch of swatches travel with me so ideas return to the studio unhurried.

    By the time I knit the first rows, the landscape lives inside the fabric, ready to comfort anyone who wraps it around their shoulders.

    Glarus field notes

    • Linth River overlook – teal & slate for gradient socks.
    • Klöntal pine forest – moss green with copper flecks.
    • Schwammhöhe ridge – rosy dusk for a lace border.
    • Niederurnen orchards – soft apricot for mittens.

    Next outing

    Planning a sunrise sketch session at Lake Klöntal with thermos chai and a basket of fresh swatches.

    Inside the Chalet Studio

    A look at the routines that keep my Glarus retirement atelier vibrant and welcoming.

    18 Nov 2023 · 5 min read

    The studio wakes at 6 a.m. when I open the shutters to watch pink light brush the Tödi peak.

    I warm the space with a small wood stove, then line up yarn cakes chosen the night before. A gratitude note sits beside the swift.

    Studio rhythms

    • 06:30 kettle + gratitude journaling.
    • 07:15 colour planning under north-facing window.
    • 09:00 dye pot check-in and stretch break.
    • 16:00 tidy surfaces, photograph progress, close the day.

    Retirement means I can move slowly; I listen to the weather and let it decide whether I dye, design, or simply mend.

    This rhythm keeps my mind clear and reminds me that craft is a companion, not a race.

    Studio essentials

    • Maple worktable built by my father.
    • Vintage swift rescued from a Basel flea market.
    • Set of 3.75–5 mm birch needles for everyday projects.
    • Playlist of Swiss jazz and the sound of snowmelt.

    Community note

    I host a monthly knit circle at Gemeindehausplatz; newcomers receive tea, pattern notes, and a hug at the door.

    My atelier · My activities

    Threads of a quiet retirement

    Four pillars guide my days, four ways to honour wool, alpine seasons and the people who wear my pieces.

    The hand

    Every piece is hand-knit on wooden needles. No factories, no shortcuts — just rows, breathing, and Swiss patience.

    The fibre

    Swiss merino, baby alpaca, kid mohair, organic cotton: I source small, traceable batches and meet many of the spinners by name.

    The seasons

    Glarus light dictates my colours — frost on the Linth in winter, larch gold in autumn, alpine teal at dusk.

    The sharing

    I host monthly knit nights, write care guides, and gift each piece with a hand-written note about its making.

    —
    Timeline

    A retirement journey, row by row

    From the first cast-on after retirement to today’s atelier — a few quiet milestones.

    2017

    First cast-on

    Retirement opens the door. I cast on a pair of socks for my eldest grandson — and never put the needles down.

    2019

    A studio of my own

    I convert the chalet attic into a tiny atelier — north light, wood stove, and a vintage swift rescued in Basel.

    2021

    Glarus knit nights

    Monthly knit circles begin at Gemeindehausplatz. Tea, patterns, and stories cross generations.

    2024

    Twelve alpine palettes

    I finalise twelve signature palettes — from Amber Honey to Misty Teal — each tied to a Glaronese place.

    The studio diary

    Short notes from the chalet attic — a yarn discovery, a sentence I underlined, a sketch of Tödi at dawn. No order, only weather.

    1. 12 Apr 2026 · Tuesday morning

      Snow returned to Tödi overnight. I drew its silhouette on the back of a tea tin and chose three skeins for today: cloud white, larch bark, summit shadow. The kitchen smells of bergamot.

      SketchesPalette
    2. 28 Mar 2026 · Saturday late

      Annie Dillard, Pilgrim at Tinker Creek: “Beauty and grace are performed whether or not we will sense them.” I knit a row of garter stitch as I read this sentence three times.

      Reading
    3. 16 Mar 2026 · Knit night

      Anneliese came back to the circle after two years away. She brought her late mother’s needles wrapped in linen. We did not knit much; we listened more.

      Encounters
    4. 22 Feb 2026 · Sunday

      Mira called from the Klöntal farm. The first March mérinos is being washed. She asked if I wanted the cream or the buttery one. Both, of course. Both, always.

      YarnEncounters
    5. 8 Feb 2026 · Sunday

      I drew the Linth river from memory while waiting for the kettle. The river never stays the same colour twice in winter — slate, teal, oxblood at noon.

      Sketches
    6. 19 Jan 2026 · Sunday

      The fog never lifted today. The whole valley pearl grey. I dyed a small batch of cream wool with eucalyptus to match — three soakings, no urgency.

      WeatherPalette
    7. 24 Dec 2025 · Christmas Eve

      I gave my eldest grandson the Heather Valley socks. He put them on immediately, shoes too tight after, didn’t care. Best gift returned: his face.

      Encounters
    8. 30 Nov 2025 · Saint Andrew’s

      Tried German short rows again on the Glärnisch shawl. The transition is finally invisible. Twelve years of knitting and a single technique can still humble you. Good.

      Technique
    9. 12 Oct 2025 · Sunday

      Re-reading Anni Albers, On Weaving. Her line: “Material is the prime mover.” I underline it in the margin with a tea-stained finger.

      Reading
    10. 8 Sep 2025 · Vinothèque

      Tasted three Glaronese wines with Madeleine. The dornfelder gave me my next palette: smoky garnet, cracked leather, evening fog.

      EncountersPalette
    11. 21 Aug 2025 · Heatwave

      Too hot to knit wool, so I turned to a linen cap. Cool grey, clean grey, river grey. The needles felt cold. A blessing.

      WeatherSketches
    12. 5 Jul 2025 · Lake Klöntal

      Kayak day with Madeleine. The lake produced exactly the teal I have been looking for since March. Took a photo, took a yarn cake, both for the studio wall.
